Overview of Healthcare Access, Expenditure, and Coverage in the US

The NHIS is the nation’s largest in-person household health survey. It has been conducted annually since 1957 by the National Center for Health Statistics (NCHS), which is a part of the Centers for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC). Interviewing is done by the U.S. Census Bureau’s field staff. During face-to-face interviews, participants provided sociodemographic data and information about health insurance coverage status.

The purpose of our research project is to explore health insurance coverage using the National Health Interview Survey (NHIS) data. A broad range of topics are covered within the dataset, but we will specifically focus on healthcare access, coverage, and expenditure across a multitude of factors such as over time and educational attainment. We explore three specific questions:

  1. Access: Does higher educational attainment cause greater chance of acquiring medical insurance?
  2. Coverage: What percentage of the population had health coverage?
  3. Expenditure: How has the amount of money spent for medical care changed over the years?

Healthcare Access

"Does higher educational attainment cause greater chance of acquiring medical insurance?"

One specific set of question asked by the field staff was the health insurance coverage status. The following visualization displays a high level overview of the percentage of people who didn't have any health insurance. According to the codebook, uninsured individuals include:

"Those without any private insurance, or without public health insurance coverage through Medicaid, Medicare, military health care (1976 forward), Children's Health Insurance Program (CHIP) (1999 forward), or a state-sponsored or other government program (1997 forward). Persons covered only by a private plan that paid for one type of service, such as accidents or dental care, are also considered uninsured. Persons covered only by the Indian Health Service (1993 forward) are coded as having no health insurance. "
Data Preparation
To explore the question above, we looked at data from the 05' to 15' NHIS surveys, downloaded from this website. Using a stratified random sample, we counted the weighted total of the population who responded "No" to having any health insurance coverage. We then compared this to to the weighted population for that respective year, ultimately getting a weighted proportion of people that respective year who didn't have health insurance, accompanied by a confidence interval.

Healthcare Expenditure

"How has the amount of money spent for medical care changed over the years?"

The NHIS survey contained various information on healthcare expenditure, such as the premiums they pay for all the individual health care plans they currently had. One specific question asked respondents the total amount of money spent for medical care, for the past year. This independent variable only provides a rough estimate on the family's expenditure on medical care, but is still relevant since interviewers directed respondents to exclude the insurance premiums, over-the-counter drugs, and any costs for which they expected to be reimbursed. A distribution of this can be seen below:

Right off the bat we could see that for the most part, respondents are spending less than $500 only for health insurance, which fluctuates between 25,000 to 40,000 people every year. The next biggest group would be the people spending $500 to $1999, fluctuating between 20,000 to 33,000 people every year.

Of course, this is just an estimate, but a statistical inference can be made on the greater population, by using survey weights provided within the NHIS dataset. A stratified random sampling was implemented to calculate our weighted proportions of health expenditure. The results can be seen below:

We see that the largest proportion of people mostly spent less than 500 dollars through out time, with atleast more than 30% given any year. Specifically, for 2005, 37.5% percent of the population spent less than 500 dollars, but for 2015, it went down to 32.04%. For the people spending no money on health insurance, this proportion mostly stayed between 8% to 12% over the years, with a positive increase over time. One would expect that with the introduction of the Affordable Care Act in 2010, which promised reducing health care costs to make it more affordable, the amount of money people are spending on health care would decrease. These expectations turned out to be true, since the graph reflects a positive rate of change for people spending no money on health insurance, and negative rate of change for people spending less than 500 dollars.
